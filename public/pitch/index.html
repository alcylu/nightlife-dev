We're not building one product — we're building the data infrastructure that powers many products. Our AI pipeline ingests from six source types: social media scraping (Instagram stories, Facebook events), ticketing platform feeds (RA, Peatix, EventBrite), venue websites and schedules, promoter and DJ networks, gated insider knowledge from door staff and dancers and regulars, and survey and feedback data. It takes chaotic, multilingual nightlife data — Japanese flyers, Instagram stories, WhatsApp group chats — and structures it into clean, queryable, monetizable feeds. The gated knowledge is the moat: you can't scrape what lives in a promoter's head or a bouncer's DM thread. And each new city makes the pipeline smarter — compounding returns. That single data layer powers five interfaces, each serving a different user: the consumer site for tourists and locals, the developer API for AI agents and travel apps, a City Agent for travelers and hotel guests, a Venue Agent that automates the messy supply side for clubs and promoters, and a white-label hotel concierge product. Each new interface is just another surface on the same data. That's the platform play.

The "Aha" Moment

The agent doesn’t just search — it knows the city, reads the room, and handles everything.

Guest: “We’re a group of 6 in Tokyo for a bachelorette party tonight. Fun, upscale, great music — we want to celebrate and dance!”
Agent calls: get_recommendations({ city: "tokyo", vibe: "upscale, danceable", party_size: 6, occasion: "bachelorette" })
Agent calls: get_vip_availability({ venues: ["celavi", "zouk", "zero"], date: "tonight" })
Agent: “Great options tonight! Charlotte de Witte is at Zero if you like techno — but only one VIP table left. For a celebration I’d suggest CeLaVi or Zouk — both have great bottle service and the vibe is perfect for bachelorettes. Want me to book?”
Guest: “Can you lock in that table at Zouk? I love Charlotte! Get us on the guest list at Zero too.”
Agent calls: create_vip_booking({ venue: "zouk", table: "A3", party_size: 6 })
Agent calls: join_guest_list({ venue: "zero", party_size: 6, name: "Sarah + group" })
Agent: “Done! Table A3 at Zouk confirmed for 11 PM — champagne tower for the bride included. You’re on the guest list at Zero for Charlotte too. Have an amazing night!”
This is the aha moment. Notice what the agent does that Google can't: it understands the occasion (bachelorette), searches for matching venues, then checks real-time VIP availability across all three results simultaneously. It knows Charlotte de Witte is playing at Zero tonight — that's gated knowledge you can't scrape from a website. It reads the room: techno is wrong for a bachelorette, so it steers toward CeLaVi and Zouk for the celebration vibe. The guest went from "we want to go out" to a VIP table booking plus backup guest list in 60 seconds. The hotel earns 10-30% commission on that table.

Venue Agent

Supply-Side

Venues don't use APIs. They gatekeep. Our AI agent talks to them so you don't have to.

THE PROBLEM
No APIs, no online booking, info locked in DMs and group chats
OUR SOLUTION
AI agent interfaces with venues via their preferred channels

Guest asks → City Agent → checks data + books via → Venue Agent → confirms with venue → Done
The guest never knows how messy the supply side is. That's the moat.
This is the full architecture. On the consumer side, we're building an AI agent for every city — not a chatbot, a genuine local expert. It has structured event data plus qualitative knowledge seeded from club promoters, DJs, venue security, dancers, performers. It knows which club has the best sound system, which night to avoid, which DJ is actually good live. And it can take action — book a VIP table, buy tickets, put you on the guest list. On the supply side, we have a second AI agent that faces the venues. Here's the thing about nightlife venues: they don't use APIs. They don't have online booking systems. Everything is gatekept — locked in DMs, WhatsApp groups, relationships. Our venue agent handles that. It talks to venues through whatever channel they prefer, gets real-time availability, negotiates pricing, confirms bookings. The guest asks one question and gets a seamless answer. They never see the messy supply-side integration. That abstraction layer is the moat — it's incredibly hard to replicate because it requires both the data infrastructure AND the venue relationships.
